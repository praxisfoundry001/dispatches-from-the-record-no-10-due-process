# Dispatches from the Record: Clerk Notes No. 10 — Due Process as Trained Civic Maintenance Against Runaway Reaction

## What is this?

This is the tenth note in the *Dispatches from the Record: Clerk Notes* series. It offers a plain-language structural reading of procedural due process.

## Why should I care?

Due process is often summarized as “fair procedure,” but that can make it sound thinner than it is. This note explains why notice, hearing, neutrality, timing, record, balancing, and review belong together when public power prepares to deprive a person of life, liberty, or property.

## What is the claim?

The central claim is that due process is the difference between civic reaction and lawful civic judgment. It is the trained maintenance structure that prevents public power from converting a protected person into a target before the system has lawfully perceived, heard, balanced, authorized, and recorded what it is doing.

## What is this not?

This is not legal advice, a replacement for procedural-due-process doctrine, a criminal-procedure guide, an administrative-law treatise, or a case-specific adequacy analysis. It also does not cover substantive due process. It offers a structural interpretation of what procedural due process is doing.

## How was this done?

The note uses the Constitutional Physiology / Structural Flow lens to read procedural due process as reaction-control architecture at the person-boundary: notice, hearing, neutrality, timing, record, balancing, review, repair, and release.
